# You Don't Have to Keep Up

It matters who you spend your thinking hours with, and in 2026 I spend most of mine with the model. Left alone it's an endlessly agreeable PA. Its first answer is usually whatever most people would say, because that's what it's made of, and a year of accepted first answers would have left me with work that looks like everyone else's.

About a year ago I started building a harness for myself, with one rule: don't get fancy. One project folder, where I'd yarn with Claude whenever I wanted to refine how we worked together. I never asked it to create anything. I'd start talking about a problem, ask a lot of questions, and spend most of the time challenging whatever either of us assumed straight away, because almost every time an assumption broke there was a better idea sitting behind it. I brought it writing that had nothing to do with AI. Sometimes a session produced a primitive and sometimes it didn't, and most of them were eventually deleted. Most of the year went into conversations with nothing at the end of them.

Like everyone, I was buried in advice about how to work with these things, and it took me a while to see why it mattered so little: the model is trained on it. Its default is the consensus of that advice, and whatever a post can teach everyone comes free with a later release. So I let the feed go. The facts I still need, like what changed in Claude Code and when, the harness verifies against each release and keeps as a snapshot. Keeping up became the harness's job.

I kept the trending vocabulary out too. A borrowed name carries the most popular version of the idea it names, and if I'd built under those names I'd have drifted into building the popular things. So I described what I wanted in the words I already had, which is why the things I build get cooked and tasted and go stale instead of getting orchestrated.

The feeling of falling behind treats all AI knowledge as one asset. I think it's two. Facts about the tools, what shipped and what the model can do now, change weekly, and they're the exact category the tools themselves are best at holding or checking on demand. Judgment about work, what good looks like in your domain and what you want, changes on the timescale of a career. The feed only carries the first kind, and the first kind depreciates fastest, because anything that can be written in a post everyone reads is generic by definition, and generic technique is what the next release swallows. Keeping up, in the ambient way we all feel we have to, is a bad investment.

There's an inversion in this that I had backwards for a while: the better the models get, the less keeping up matters. Weak tools reward technique, the tricks and workarounds and incantations, so early on currency paid. As the tool approaches competence the technique evaporates, because the model meets you where you are. Most of us respond to the speed by clinging harder to the feed, and the truth runs the other way. The real requirement was never being up to date; it's being able to get up to date in an hour when a decision needs it. That used to be expensive. Now it's nearly free, which is what makes the ambient version pointless.

The honest caveat is that this is easier to say with a career of judgment behind me. But I don't think the beginner's version is keep up more. The feed feels like learning and is mostly spectating; judgment comes from doing work and being wrong about it. You don't need to keep up, you need reps.

At some point I asked the model, mid-argument, what's actually left for a person once it has read everything. Its answer, close to verbatim:

> I'm trained on roughly everything everyone wrote down, so what's left has a short answer: whatever was never written down anywhere. Your unarticulated preferences. The local truths of one team or one codebase. Things that happened after the training run. And your reaction to my output, which can't exist before the output does. Everything else I have or will have.

The deep item on that list is wanting. The model holds everyone's wants averaged together; that's what a median is. Ask it for something and the want-shaped hole gets filled with the average want, competently. Your actual want isn't in there. Often it isn't in you either, not in words. People mostly can't say what they want up front, and can always tell you when it's wrong.

That's why the yarning worked, and why it had to be mostly waste. A want moves from tacit to explicit through friction, not introspection: no, not that, closer, why did you do that. A year of arguments with no deliverable was that friction run daily, and the harness is just where whatever survived it lives. My own taste went in on the same terms as everything else: each opinion I write down becomes a test the bare model takes on every release, and the ones it catches up to get deleted. The survivors are rarely the ones I held hardest. I never had to take my own word for any of it.

There are a lot of talented people making the model better every few months, and they don't need my help. What I want, it can only get from me. From what I can see, that's where these tools are headed: models very good at conversing with, understanding, and building on whatever a person brings them. Which means the bottleneck, eventually, is what you bring. Not the model, and not your knowledge of it.

That's what the year distilled into: a place where what I want lives in a form an agent can use, plus the machinery for deleting whatever stops earning its keep. It doesn't ask for anyone else's directives; the model already knows what everyone knows. I called it [agent-kitchen](https://github.com/claudialnathan/agent-kitchen), and nothing in it is fancy. That was the rule.
